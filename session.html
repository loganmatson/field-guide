act 1 · the hook

Stop renting your effort.
Start compounding it.

The status update, the reformatted report, the email about the meeting: effort you re-spend every week and keep nothing from. Rented effort evaporates. Configured effort compounds. Do the work once, teach it, and it keeps paying every week after. This session is about making the switch.

act 1 · the mental model

A sous-chef who knows your kitchen.

The fastest way to explain the whole thing to anyone. Thirty seconds.

Every other AI tool
The visiting chef

Walks into a kitchen they have never seen, answers one question, and leaves. Tomorrow you start over from zero. A blank slate, every single conversation.

Claude Code
Your sous-chef

Remembers the pantry. Knows last week's rule. Knows which knife is sharp. Shows up in your folder every day and gets faster each week, because the kitchen is always the same.

The ladder below is just names for how deep the kitchen goes.

analogy

the ladder · rung 3 of 5
Agents

A skill with judgment. It runs other skills, makes decisions, and takes action without being asked for each step.

example

Yours, probably: it notices it is Friday, gathers the numbers, drafts the report, and queues the send.

Mine, really: my meal-planner reads the training week and plans seven days of food with zero questions.

analogy

A thermostat reads the room. An agent feels the cold front coming and heats the house before you wake.

the ladder · rung 4 of 5
Pipeline

Agents chained in sequence, each handing its output to the next. One command runs the entire line.

example

Yours, probably: numbers in, clean data out, analysis done, report drafted, email sent. Nobody touched the middle.

Mine, really: /optimize-week runs six agents: training, risk, meals, groceries, calendar, email.

analogy

An assembly line. Every station does one thing well, and the finished car rolls off the end.

the ladder · rung 5 of 5
Empire

The whole system. Dozens of skills, multiple agents and pipelines, one shared memory, all compounding.

example

Yours, eventually: every workflow your team repeats, captured once, running weekly, sharper every correction.

Mine, really: 62 skills, 6 agents, 6 pipelines on one shared memory. It audits itself monthly.

analogy

A city. Roads, power, districts: built once, and every new building plugs into all of it.

Speed versus leverage. You watched it run twice already.

"It forgets everything between chats."
actually

Instruction files, a memory index, and per-skill notes persist across sessions. Week ten beats week one because every correction accumulates.

"It's a black box. I can't trust it."
actually

Plan mode keeps it read-only until you approve. Every change is a visible diff, every session a transcript. You watched the risk guard pause the run. That pause is a setting.

"This must cost a fortune."
actually

About one software seat per person per month. The pipelines you watched cost cents per run. The real investment is configuration time, which pays back weekly.

the exercise · your turn

Build your own first working skill.

Two moves. First you watch a skill that already ships in the box explain itself, so you see what one is. Then the first skill you ever make is the one that interviews you before every build. Why this one first? It is the gate every future skill passes through. Get it right once and everything you build after it starts from a clean spec instead of a vague idea. Ten minutes, and it is yours for good.

⛑ If the build misfires: paste this and keep going

Auth hiccup, a timeout, a model that wanders? You never lose the demo. This is a complete, working interview skill. Paste it where step 4 told you, then fire it. Each line is one part of the anatomy.

# save as ~/.claude/skills/interview/SKILL.md (or paste into Project knowledge) --- name: interview description: Use before any new build, skill, or agent. Interviews the user, then writes a SPEC. --- # Interview Before writing anything, interview the user about what they want to build. 1. Read the conversation above for context first. Do not re-ask what you already know. 2. Ask at least 10 questions, ONE at a time, and adapt each question to the last answer. No fixed script. 3. Track your own confidence (0-100%). Keep asking until you hit 99%. 4. Ask where the output should be saved. Offer 2-3 likely paths. 5. Only then write a SPEC.md. Never start building during the interview. Spec only.

That is the whole skill. A name, a description that says when to fire, numbered steps, and one hard limit. Everything else you build follows the same shape.
