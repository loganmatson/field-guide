act 1 · the hook

Stop renting your effort.
Start compounding it.

The status update, the reformatted report, the email about the meeting: effort you re-spend every week and keep nothing from. Rented effort evaporates. Configured effort compounds. Do the work once, teach it, and it keeps paying every week after. This session is about making the switch.

the ladder · rung 1 of 5
Workflows

Any multi-step process you repeat by hand. The raw material everything else here is built from.

example

Yours, probably: every Friday you pull the numbers, reformat the deck, and email the summary.

Mine, really: planning my entire training week by hand, every Sunday night.

analogy

the ladder · rung 2 of 5
Skills

A workflow taught to Claude once: what to do, when it fires, what it must never touch. Reused on command.

example

Yours, probably: a status update that writes itself the way you would write it.

Mine, really: my writing skill drafts emails in my voice, trained on five real samples.

analogy

A sous-chef who knows your kitchen. Trained once, reliable every night, never relearns your spice rack.

the ladder · rung 3 of 5
Agents

A skill with judgment. It runs other skills, makes decisions, and takes action without being asked for each step.

example

Yours, probably: it notices it is Friday, gathers the numbers, drafts the report, and queues the send.

Mine, really: my meal-planner reads the training week and plans seven days of food with zero questions.

analogy

A thermostat reads the room. An agent feels the cold front coming and heats the house before you wake.

the ladder · rung 4 of 5
Pipeline

Agents chained in sequence, each handing its output to the next. One command runs the entire line.

example

Yours, probably: numbers in, clean data out, analysis done, report drafted, email sent. Nobody touched the middle.

Mine, really: /optimize-week runs six agents: training, risk, meals, groceries, calendar, email.

analogy

An assembly line. Every station does one thing well, and the finished car rolls off the end.

the ladder · rung 5 of 5
Empire

The whole system. Dozens of skills, multiple agents and pipelines, one shared memory, all compounding.

example

Yours, eventually: every workflow your team repeats, captured once, running weekly, sharper every correction.

Mine, really: 44 skills, 6 agents, 5 pipelines on one shared memory. It audits itself monthly.

analogy

A city. Roads, power, districts: built once, and every new building plugs into all of it.

act 2 · the fair questions

The fair questions, answered.

"It's just a better Copilot."
actually

Copilot autocompletes the line you are typing. This takes the whole task and does it end to end while you do something else. Speed versus leverage.

"It forgets everything between chats."
actually

Instruction files, a memory index, and per-skill notes persist across sessions. Week ten beats week one because every correction accumulates.

"It's a black box. I can't trust it."
actually

Plan mode keeps it read-only until you approve. Every change is a visible diff, every session a transcript. You watched the risk guard pause the run. That pause is a setting.

"This must cost a fortune."
actually

About one software seat per person per month. The pipelines you watched cost cents per run. The real investment is configuration time, which pays back weekly.

the exercise · your turn

Build your own first working skill.

Two moves. First you watch a skill that already ships in the box explain itself, so you see what one is. Then the first skill you ever make is the one that interviews you before every build. Ten minutes, and it is yours for good.

part a

Watch a skill explain itself · 60 seconds

  1. 1
    Open Claude Code. Terminal, type claude, hit enter.
  2. 2
    Look under the hood. Nothing gets built here; a skill that already ships in the box introduces itself. Say:
    Explain what a skill is by showing me one that's already installed: open the skill-creator skill and walk me through its parts.
    That file pattern (a name, a description, instructions, limits) is all a skill is.
part b

Your first skill is the interviewer

  1. 3
    Commission it. Same session. Say:
    Use skill-creator to build me a skill called interview. Requirements: before any new build, it interviews me with at least 10 questions, asked one at a time with the AskUserQuestion tool. The questions adapt to my previous answers, no fixed script. It reads the conversation above for context before asking anything. It asks where I want the output saved. It only writes a SPEC.md once it is 99% confident it understands what I want.
  2. 4
    Fire it.
    Have it running? Type /interview + something you actually want built. No account yet? Tonight. Both say-cards work in a fresh session.
