Part 02 · Team Workspace

Set up Claude CoWork for your team

A start-to-finish guide for getting your whole team on Claude — shared skills, shared knowledge, and admin controls. No code, no terminal. Just click through, top to bottom.

🏢
Think of CoWork as a shared office in software form. One room, your whole team inside, and a brilliant assistant (Claude) at every desk — already briefed on how your company works, with the same playbook open in front of everyone.
💬 Just Claude.ai

Personal chat. Great for one person. Nothing is shared — every teammate starts from scratch.

🏢 CoWork (this guide)

A team workspace. Shared knowledge, shared skills in everyone's / menu, and admin controls so you know exactly what it can do.

💳 Pick your plan

Two plans support team workspaces. Here's the short version — full pricing lives on Anthropic's site.

If you can name everyone on the team by heart, start with Team. Move to Enterprise when you need single sign-on, role-based permissions, or your security team wants an audit trail. You can upgrade later without losing anything.

🗺️ The setup, one level at a time

Seven phases, in order. Each one builds on the last. Click any phase to open it — skim the summaries first, then drill into the one you're on.

1
Level 1

Admin Console Foundation

Confirm you're the admin and your Team plan is live.
›
1.1 Confirm you're the admin

Log into claude.ai → click your profile photo (top right) → Settings → Organization. If you see an Admin section in the left nav, you're the admin.

You're probably not the account owner yet. Ask whoever first created the Claude account to grant you admin access — or reach out to support.claude.com. Nothing else in this guide works until you're an admin.
1.2 Confirm the Team plan is active

Settings → Billing → your plan should read "Team." If it says "Pro" or individual, upgrade via Billing → Upgrade → Team (minimum 5 seats).

1.3 Set up SSO / domain capture (recommended)

Settings → Security → Domain Capture. Enter your company email domain (e.g. yourcompany.com). Anyone who signs up with that domain gets auto-joined — no shadow accounts. If you use Google Workspace or Microsoft 365, enable SSO here too.

1.4 Decide if Public projects should exist at all (recommended if you handle sensitive data)

Organization settings → Data and privacy → find Public projects and toggle it off. (Primary Owner / Owner only.) This doesn't turn off sharing — it just removes the "visible to the whole org" option. People can still share a project with specific named teammates; they just can't make one browsable by everyone.

Flip this off and any existing org-wide ("Public") projects convert to Private automatically — nothing is deleted, nobody loses access they already had. New projects default to private-by-invite instead of org-browsable. Fully reversible from the same toggle later.
Domain capture is the difference between a tidy org and a mess. Without it, people sign up with personal emails, create their own little orgs, and your team data scatters. Capture the domain once and everyone lands in the same place automatically. Deciding on Public projects now does the same job for visibility: it's much easier to start locked-down and open up deliberately than to discover six months in that a sensitive project was browsable by the whole company.
Questions on this step
🙅 I don't see an Admin section — am I stuck? +
It means you're not the account owner yet. Ask whoever first created the Claude account to grant you admin, or contact support.claude.com. Nothing else in this guide works until you're an admin.
Next → Once you have admin access, confirm the Team plan is active (1.2).
🌐 Do I have to set up SSO and domain capture now? +
It's recommended, not required. You can invite people manually and add domain capture later — but doing it now prevents shadow accounts where teammates sign up with personal emails and scatter your data into separate orgs.
Next → Whether or not you enable SSO, decide on Public projects (1.4), then invite the team.
🔓 If I turn off Public projects, do I lose anything? +
No. Existing org-wide projects convert to Private automatically — nothing is deleted and nobody loses access they already had. People can still share with specific named teammates; they just can't make a project browsable by the whole company. Fully reversible later.
Next → Move on to inviting your team in Level 2.
2
Level 2

Invite Your Team

Send invites, assign seats, set spending caps.
›
2.1 Invite members

Settings → Members → Invite Members. Enter each person's work email — they get an invite link. Assign seats:

  • Standard seat — CoWork, Chat, Projects, Skills. Enough for most teams.
  • Premium seat — higher usage limits + priority access, for heavy daily users.
2.2 Set spending caps (optional)

Settings → Billing → Spending Controls. Set a per-user cap so one heavy user can't burn the team budget. Default is pooled org-wide usage.

🪑
Seats are like desks in your office. Standard desks suit almost everyone; a few power users get the corner office (Premium). Count your desks before the team arrives so nobody's left standing.
Inviting people is what turns a personal account into a team. But the seat and cap choices here are also your budget guardrails — set them now and you never get a surprise bill later.
Questions on this step
🪑 Standard or Premium seat — how do I decide? +
Start almost everyone on Standard; it covers CoWork, Chat, Projects, and Skills. Give Premium (higher limits, priority access) only to the handful of people who'll use it heavily every day. You can change a seat later if someone outgrows it.
Next → Assign seats, then set spending caps in 2.2.
💸 Will one heavy user blow the budget? +
Not if you set a per-user cap under Billing → Spending Controls. By default usage is pooled org-wide; a per-user cap stops any single person from burning the whole team's budget.
Next → With caps set, turn on Skills org-wide in Level 3.
📧 A teammate didn't get their invite. +
Check the email was entered correctly and look in their spam. You can resend from Settings → Members. If your domain capture is on (1.3), they may be able to just sign up with their work email and auto-join instead.
Next → Once everyone's in, move to turning on Skills.
3
Level 3

Turn On Skills, Org-Wide

Unlock the / menu and push ready-made skills to everyone.
›
3.1 Turn skills on

Settings → Organization → Skills → toggle Enable Skills for Organization → On. This unlocks the / menu for everyone. Without it, the / shortcut does nothing.

3.2 Two ways skills reach people
  • Path A — they build their own. Fine for power users, risky for a team.
  • Path B — you push from the top. You upload a skill once; it appears in every member's / menu automatically. This is your path.
3.3 Create skills with the built-in Skill Creator

Open the desktop app → CoWork tab → type / → pick Skill Creator. It runs a guided interview and writes the whole skill for you. Then make it visible: Settings → Organization → Skills → Visible to Organization → On.

Document Review — flags important language and asks before drawing conclusions. Drafting Assistant — proposes wording for you to approve, never sends anything on its own. Both are safe, high-value starters that show the team what "good" looks like.
📲
Pushing skills top-down is like IT installing the same app on every company laptop. Nobody hunts for it, nobody installs the wrong version — it's just there in everyone's menu on day one.
3.4 What your teammates will see

Once you've pushed a skill, here's what to tell the team: open any chat or CoWork session, type /, and look for the skill by name in the list that appears. No install, no setup on their end — if it's marked Visible to Organization, it's already sitting in their menu. Selecting it runs the skill on whatever they're working on.

  • Don't see it? Settings → Capabilities → Skills should show Skills: On on their account — most "missing skill" reports trace back to this toggle.
  • Still missing after that? It likely isn't marked Visible to Organization yet — that's an admin-side fix, not theirs.
Skills are where a generic assistant becomes your team's assistant. Pushing them top-down means everyone gets the same vetted, tested workflows — no rogue prompts, no "it works on my machine."
Questions on this step
🆚 Should people build their own skills or should I push them? +
For a team, push from the top. You build and vet a skill once, mark it Visible to Organization, and it appears in everyone's / menu automatically — no rogue versions. Let power users build their own only after the shared set is in place.
Next → Build your first skill with the Skill Creator (3.3).
🛠️ I'm not technical — can I actually make a skill? +
Yes. The built-in Skill Creator runs a guided interview and writes the whole skill for you — no code. Good first two: a Document Review skill and a Drafting Assistant. Both are safe, high-value, and show the team what "good" looks like.
Next → After building it, set it Visible to Organization, then build the shared Project.
👻 A teammate can't see the skill I pushed. +
Two usual causes: their account has Settings → Capabilities → Skills turned off (their fix), or the skill isn't marked Visible to Organization yet (your admin-side fix). Check the org toggle first — most "missing skill" reports trace back to it.
Next → Confirm visibility, then move on to the shared Project in Level 4.
4
Level 4

Create the Shared Project

One shared “briefcase” every teammate's session reads from.
›
💼
A Project is a shared briefcase. Drop in your policies, templates, and reference docs once — and every teammate's Claude opens that same briefcase before it answers a single question.
4.1 Create it

Main Claude interface (not Settings) → left sidebar → Projects → New Project. Name it clearly, e.g. Compliance Workflows 2026.

4.2 Add Project Knowledge

Inside the project → Project Knowledge → Add Documents. Upload your manuals, templates, and a short context.md you write yourself — 1–2 pages on what the team does and what Claude should always know.

4.3 Add custom instructions

Inside the project → Customize Claude → write standing rules. For example:

You are assisting our team.
Always ask before suggesting interpretations.
Never finalize anything without flagging it for human review.
Default to Ask mode — propose actions, don't execute them.
Cite your sources.
4.4 Decide who can see it

Inside the project → click Share (next to the project name). Under General access you have two choices:

  • Only people invited (Private) — the default. Nobody sees it until you add them by name or email, one at a time or pasted in bulk.
  • Everyone at [your org] (Public) — anyone in the organization can find it in the Team tab and start using it. (Only available if your admin hasn't disabled it — see step 1.4.)

When you invite someone to a private project, pick their permission level: Can view (use it, chat in it, can't change it) or Can edit (can modify the project's knowledge and instructions — for everyone on it). Default to Can view unless someone genuinely needs to maintain it.

4.5 The real pattern most teams need: several private projects, not one shared one

"Shared with the whole org" is rarely what you actually want. The far more common need is sharing this project with these specific people — one client engagement, a sensitive workflow, a subset of the team — without opening it to everyone else. Decide each project's audience on purpose:

  • Org-wide knowledge (general policies, templates everyone uses) → Public, or Private shared with the whole team.
  • Team-specific work (one department's workflows) → Private, invite just that team.
  • Narrow or sensitive work (one client, one engagement, a handful of people) → Private, invite only those names, and lean toward Can view over Can edit.

Same feature, three different invite lists — most orgs end up running several private projects side by side rather than one project shared with everyone.

4.6 What your teammates will see

If it's Public (or Private and shared with the whole team), it shows up under Projects in the left sidebar — look for the name you gave it (e.g. Compliance Workflows 2026). If it's Private and you invited specific people, it lands in their Projects → Shared with you tab instead, and they get an email the moment you add them. Either way, every chat started from inside it automatically reads the Project Knowledge and follows the custom instructions.

  • Don't see it anywhere? Confirm you were actually added to the invite list (for Private) or that it's set to Public — that's the most common reason it's missing.
  • Project Knowledge (files, notes) is visible to everyone who has access — that's the point. Chats are not — conversations inside the project stay private to whoever started them unless that person separately clicks Share on that chat. Worth saying out loud before someone assumes otherwise.
This is the heart of a team setup. Without a shared project, every person is teaching Claude from zero. With it, the newest hire's Claude is as well-briefed as the founder's — instantly.

Usually several. "Shared with the whole org" is rarely what you want — most teams run a few private projects side by side: org-wide knowledge open to everyone, team-specific work invited to that team, and sensitive or single-client work locked to a handful of names. Same feature, three different invite lists.
Next → Decide each project's audience on purpose (4.5), then set guardrails in Level 5.

Next → Confirm sharing is set how you want, then lock the guardrails.

5
Level 5

Lock the Guardrails

Enforce “Ask mode” so nothing happens without approval.
›
5.1 Enforce Ask mode

This small config file turns off "Auto mode" for everyone and stops them re-enabling it. It goes on each employee's machine as managed-mcp.json:

{
  "permissions": {
    "disableBypassPermissionsMode": "disable",
    "disableAutoMode": "disable"
  }
}
  • Best: push it via your MDM (Intune, Jamf, etc.) to %APPDATA%\Claude\managed-mcp.json. Fully enforced.
  • No MDM? Have each person create the file manually, or forward this section to your IT contact.
disableBypassPermissionsMode means employees can't switch off the approval prompts. disableAutoMode grays out Auto mode entirely — Ask mode becomes the only option. Together: nothing happens on your data without a human clicking "approve."
5.2 Verify on one machine first

Before rolling out to everyone, check on a single machine: skill menu shows your skills, Auto mode is grayed out, shared project is visible. All good? Roll it out.

🔑
Think of this like the key on a forklift. The machine is powerful and useful — but it only moves when a trained operator turns the key. Ask mode is that key.
This is the line that lets you say "we use AI" and "we know exactly what it does, and we can prove it." For any team handling sensitive data, that sentence is the whole point.
Questions on this step
🏢 We have no MDM — can I still enforce Ask mode? +
Yes, just less centrally. With MDM (Intune, Jamf) you push managed-mcp.json to every machine and it's fully enforced. Without it, have each person create the file manually, or forward step 5.1 to your IT contact to roll out.
Next → However you deploy it, verify on one machine before rolling out (5.2).
⚙️ What do those two config lines actually do? +
disableBypassPermissionsMode stops employees from switching off the approval prompts; disableAutoMode grays out Auto mode entirely so Ask mode is the only option. Together: nothing touches your data without a human clicking "approve."
Next → Confirm Auto mode is grayed out on a test machine, then roll out.
✅ How do I know it actually worked? +
Check one machine before rolling out to everyone: the skill menu shows your pushed skills, Auto mode is grayed out, and the shared project is visible. If all three look right, you're safe to deploy team-wide.
Next → Verified? Set up the three memory layers in Level 6.
6
Level 6

Memory

Three stacking layers — individual, declared, and shared team memory.
›

"Memory" in CoWork is three separate things, and they stack. Each layer costs a little more effort and pays back more in return — climb as far as your team needs. Start at Layer 1; most teams want all three.

Layer 1 · 30 seconds
Individual Memory
Claude remembers each person across sessions. One toggle.
Layer 2 · 2 minutes
Global Instructions
A permanent profile — who you are, how to work with you.
Layer 3 · automatic
Project Memory
Shared team memory that builds itself as you use the project.
🧠 Individual Memory
Effort: 30 seconds, once · Payoff: Claude stops forgetting who you are between chats

When this is on, Claude keeps a running summary of each person — their role, what they're working on, how they like answers formatted — and carries it into every future session. No more re-introducing yourself every morning.

1 Turn it on

Open claude.ai → profile photo (top right) → Settings → Capabilities → Memory → toggle On.

2 Tell it what to remember (optional)

You don't have to do anything — it learns as you go. But you can seed it: just say in a chat, "Remember that I always want options before you take action," and it files that away.

3 Review or clear it anytime

Back in Settings → Capabilities → Memory you can read everything Claude has remembered and delete any item. Nothing is hidden — it's your summary, you control it.

Every single person on the team should flip this on day one. It's the lowest-effort, highest-return setting in the whole product — the difference between an assistant that knows you and one that meets you fresh every time.
📌 Global Instructions
Effort: 2 minutes, once · Payoff: a permanent profile loaded before every session, forever

Where Individual Memory is what Claude noticed, Global Instructions are what you declared. It's a short, standing brief — your role, your rules, your house style — pinned to the top of every conversation you start. This is the layer that makes Claude feel like it works for you.

1 Open Global Instructions

Settings → CoWork → Global Instructions. (On some plans it lives under Settings → Profile → Personal Preferences — same thing, different label.)

2 Fill in the template

Run this short interview with yourself (or each teammate), then paste the filled-in version. Keep it under ~8 lines — it loads every time, so tight beats long:

I am a [role] at [company].
I primarily work with [document types].
Never [the thing] without checking with me first.
Format responses as [bullets / short / paragraphs].
Always flag [legal/regulatory language] for human review.
When drafting, assume the audience is [who].
Always present options before taking action.
3 Save and test

Save, then open a fresh chat and ask something normal. You should feel the difference immediately — it answers in your format, in your voice, without being told.

Set it once when you onboard, and revisit it only when your role or rules change. Think of Layer 1 as the memory that grows on its own, and Layer 2 as the constitution you write by hand — the non-negotiables that should never drift.
🤝 Project Memory
Effort: nothing — automatic · Payoff: the team's shared knowledge compounds the more you use it

Layers 1 and 2 are personal. This one is shared. Inside the team Project you built in Phase 4, Claude quietly builds a collective memory — the recurring questions, the patterns, the answers that worked. Everyone on the project benefits from what everyone else has already taught it.

1 There's nothing to switch on

It's built into shared Projects. As long as your team works inside the shared Project (Phase 4), Claude accumulates project memory automatically. The work is the setup.

2 Give it a few weeks

Early on, it's thin. After the team has run real work through the project, it starts to anticipate — surfacing the right template, recalling how you handled a similar case last month, answering the FAQ before anyone asks.

3 Keep the sensitive stuff out

Because this memory is shared with everyone on the project, treat it like a shared drive: anything that lands in the project can inform answers for the whole team. Great for playbooks and templates — not the place for one client's confidential file. (See the FAQ on confidential data.)

🌱
Project Memory is the layer that compounds. Layers 1 and 2 are set-and-forget. This one gets better the more your team uses it — the newest hire inherits months of the team's accumulated context the day they join the project.
🧠 One more kind of memory — the live conversation itself

The three layers above all persist between sessions. But inside any single conversation there's a faster, temporary memory too: the context window — everything Claude is holding right now in this chat. Two things worth knowing, because they come up the moment someone has a long working session:

  • It's finite. Each conversation has a ceiling — your messages, Claude's replies, and any files in play all share that space. It's working memory, not storage; it resets when you start a new chat (which is exactly why Memory, Global Instructions, and Project Memory exist to carry things over).
  • When it fills, it compacts. Instead of cutting off, Claude summarizes the earlier part of a long conversation and keeps going — so a marathon session doesn't hit a wall. The thread continues; the older detail is condensed, not lost.
🗜️
Think desk vs. filing cabinet. The context window is the desk — fast, limited, cleared between sessions. The three memory layers are the filing cabinet — permanent, carried forward. Compaction is tidying the desk mid-task: sweeping the clutter into a neat summary so there's room to keep working, without losing what matters.
For day-to-day work you won't think about this — it just works. It only matters to understand the distinction: the conversation is temporary and self-managing; Memory, Global Instructions, and the shared Project are the durable layers that make sure nothing important depends on a single chat staying open.

Level 7

Connect Your Tools

Wire in Drive, Gmail, Slack, and more — when you're ready.
›
7.1 Add connectors

Settings → Integrations → connect what your team actually uses:

  • Google Drive — Claude reads Drive docs directly
  • Gmail — pull email context into CoWork
  • Google Calendar — scheduling context
  • Slack — conversation context
  • Microsoft 365 — SharePoint, Outlook, Teams

These are enabled org-wide; each person authorizes their own account on first use.

Start with the one tool the team lives in (usually Drive or Slack). Get comfortable, then add more. Every connector you add is a door — open them deliberately, one at a time.
Questions on this step

Just one: the tool your team actually lives in, usually Google Drive or Slack. Get comfortable with how Claude uses it, then add more. Every connector is a door — open them deliberately, one at a time, not all at once.

🔑 Do I authorize connectors for the whole team? +
You enable each connector org-wide under Settings → Integrations, but each person authorizes their own account on first use — so Claude only ever sees the Drive or Gmail that individual already has access to. No one inherits anyone else's files.

Yes — there's a Microsoft 365 connector for SharePoint, Outlook, and Teams, alongside the Google and Slack ones. Turn on whichever stack your team runs on.

🙋 Questions you'll get asked

Tap any question. These are the ones every team raises on day one.

🔒 Can Anthropic see our data? +
Your team's conversations and uploaded files are used to power your workspace, not to train models, on Team and Enterprise plans. Files you upload to a Project live in your workspace. For the exact, current terms, point your security team to Anthropic's data-handling and trust documentation — and on Enterprise you get the strongest controls (SSO, retention settings, audit).
🗂️ What if someone uses the wrong tab? +
This is exactly why Phase 5 matters. With Ask mode enforced, even if someone wanders into the wrong mode, nothing executes without an approval click. For teams that should never touch code, you keep them in CoWork and the guardrails do the rest.
📄 What happens to documents we upload? +
They live in your workspace's Project Knowledge, scoped to whoever the Project is shared with. Build team artifacts inside the shared Project — that's the only path to proper team sharing with access scoping. A file in a personal session isn't shareable until it's moved into the shared Project.
🧠 Where does memory actually live — and is confidential client data safe? +
Individual Memory and Global Instructions (Layers 1–2) are tied to each person's account in your workspace, not shared. Project Memory (Layer 3) is shared with everyone who has access to that Project — and "everyone" might mean the whole org, a handful of named people, or just one collaborator, depending on how you scoped it in step 4.4. One gotcha to flag for any team handling confidential files: if your company syncs Desktop/Documents to OneDrive or Microsoft Teams/SharePoint, any files you stage locally before uploading may sync to your corporate cloud — a separate copy outside Claude. That's usually fine, but if you coach or advise clients under confidentiality, confirm your IT/data-handling policy first, and keep one client's sensitive material in a Private project invited only to the people who actually need it. The rule of thumb: a Project's memory is shared with whoever's on its invite list — choose that list deliberately, every time.
🔍 How do my teammates actually find the shared skills and project? +
Skills: type / in any chat — pushed skills just appear in the list, no setup needed (see step 3.4). Shared project: it shows up in the left sidebar under Projects, named whatever you called it (see step 4.5). Both require nothing on the teammate's end beyond having Skills turned on for their account — if either is missing, it's almost always a visibility toggle on the admin side, not something broken for that person.
🤝 Do employees have to use this? +
That's a culture call, not a technical one. The smoothest rollouts make it opt-in with one or two genuinely useful skills first, then let word of mouth do the work. Forcing it tends to backfire; showing one person saving an hour a week tends to spread on its own.
